A DIET TO LAST US THE AGES

There has long been talk over the sustainability of the food we eat. With food production and waste contributing to a third of Greenhouse Gas Emissions (GHG) today, significant degradation of our natural ecosystems and a loss of biodiversity worldwide it is imperative that action be taken. In doing so while eliminating food waste, eating locally and restructuring food production supply chains are crucial actions, simply making changes to our everyday diet can bring about great change. I aim to answer how this can be done by looking at country-level data.

It is evident from the data above that animal-products such as meat and dairy are some of the largest contributors to environmental damage. Taking account of figures like these as well as growth in global food consumption a group of scientists put forth the EAT-Lancet diet.

The EAT-Lancet diet aims to balance the growing population of the world while sticking to the confines of planetary boundaries and human health requirements. With the average diet varying considerably around the world it is interesting to see what each country must do to move towards this “ideal consumption”.
